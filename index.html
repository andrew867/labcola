Step-by-Step Process

This is the heart of the secret formula. Mix all essential oils in the exact proportions listed:

45.8 mL Lemon Oil
36.5 mL Lime Oil
1.2 mL Orange Oil
8 mL Tea Tree Oil
4.5 mL Cassia Cinnamon Oil
2.7 mL Nutmeg Oil
0.7 mL Coriander Oil
0.6 mL Fenchol

Pro Tip: Let this mixture age for 1-2 days before proceeding. This aging step was included even in the original 1886 Pemberton recipe!

Dilute the flavor oil in alcohol to make it water-dispersible:

20 mL 7X Flavor Oil + 95% Ethanol → 1L total volume

The final alcohol content in your soda will be 5x less than non-alcoholic beer—completely negligible! If alcohol is a concern, you can emulsify the oil with gum Arabic instead.

Mix all non-sugar ingredients in ~200mL hot water, then dilute to 1L:

  1. Add each ingredient slowly, allowing full dissolution
  2. Order: vinegar, caffeine, glycerin, phosphoric acid, tannins, vanilla, caramel color
  3. Dilute to exactly 1 liter with water
  4. Store in a glass bottle
85% phosphoric acid is corrosive! Wear gloves and eye protection.

For 1 liter of finished soda:

  1. 1. Dissolve 104g cane sugar in minimal water in a glass dish
  2. 2. Add 10 mL water-based solution
  3. 3. Add 1 mL 7X solution (add a few extra drops to taste)
  4. 4. Heat until nearly boiling (microwave or stovetop)
  5. 5. Cover immediately to prevent flavor loss
  6. 6. Let cool completely

Why heat? Heating causes phosphoric acid to break down limonene and citral, and converts sucrose (cane sugar) into glucose and fructose. This creates the authentic flavor profile!

  1. 1. Dilute cooled syrup to 1 liter with cold carbonated water
  2. 2. Store in refrigerator for 24 hours for best flavor
  3. 3. Serve over ice and enjoy your Lab-Cola!
Do NOT carbonate the finished soda in a SodaStream—it creates excessive foam that can harbor bacteria! Always carbonate plain water first, then mix with syrup.

The Science Behind Lab-Cola

Mass Spectrometry Analysis

Using gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS), the major flavor compounds in Coca-Cola were identified:

α-Terpineol (Dominant)

Forms from acid-catalyzed hydrolysis of limonene and pinene in citrus oils

Limonene

Primary citrus oil component, fresh lemon-lime character

Cinnamaldehyde

Main component of cinnamon oil, spicy-sweet notes

Furfural

From caramel color, adds depth and complexity

Enantiomers Tell the Story

Linalool exists in two mirror forms: R and S. Analysis revealed:

S-Linalool (Majority): Found in coriander oil ✓

R-Linalool (Trace): Found in lavender/neroli ✗

This proves coriander is IN the formula, while lavender and neroli are NOT!

The Tannin Discovery

The breakthrough came from understanding coca leaf extract as essentially a type of tea:

  • Tannins provide dry, astringent taste that masks sweetness
  • Non-volatile compounds don't show up in gas-phase mass spec
  • Wine tannins (8g/L) replicate the mouthfeel perfectly
  • This was the missing piece that made replicas taste like "diet" versions

Chemical Reactions During Heating

Sucrose Hydrolysis

Sucrose + H2O + H3PO4 → Glucose + Fructose

104g sucrose converts to ~116g simple sugars, matching corn syrup sweetness

Terpene Transformation

Limonene and citral break down under acidic conditions, creating α-terpineol and other flavor compounds

Ethyl Levulinate Formation

Fructose + heat + acid → levulinic acid esters (detected in mass spec)

Equipment & Techniques

Precision Measurement

Adjustable micropipettes (up to 1000µL) ensure accurate dosing of essential oils at the microliter level

Controlled Heating

Heat-resistant glassware prevents metal contamination from phosphoric acid while enabling crucial chemical reactions

Fun Facts

  • Vinegar is a secret ingredient! Acetic acid (from 5% vinegar) is the 3rd most prominent flavor by mass—about 10mL per batch.
  • Tea tree oil mimics coca leaf! With 50% 4-terpineol, it recreates the fresh, green notes without controlled substances.
  • The mass spectra are nearly identical! Lab-Cola's chemical fingerprint matches authentic Coca-Cola remarkably well.
  • Coca-Cola never patented their recipe! Patents require public disclosure, so they rely on trade secret protection instead.
